Traveling with a power bank is one of the most common sources of confusion at airport security. You may be surprised to know that the answer is nuanced — power banks **are** allowed on planes, but with important restrictions.

## The Short Answer

**Power banks must be in your carry-on bag.** They are **not permitted in checked luggage**. This applies to every airline worldwide.

## Why Can't Power Banks Go in Checked Bags?

Power banks contain lithium-ion batteries, which can short-circuit and cause fires if damaged or improperly stored in the cargo hold. Because the cargo hold is not easily accessible during flight, a fire there would be extremely dangerous. The cabin, by contrast, allows flight crew to address any battery incidents immediately.

This isn't an airline preference — it's an international aviation regulation mandated by IATA and enforced by agencies like the FAA and CAAS.

## The Watt-Hour Rule

The critical number to know is **100 Wh (watt-hours)**. Here's the breakdown:

| Watt-Hours | Status |
|---|---|
| Up to 100 Wh | ✅ Permitted in carry-on without approval |
| 101–160 Wh | ⚠️ Requires airline approval |
| Over 160 Wh | ❌ Prohibited on commercial flights |

### How to Calculate Watt-Hours

Most power banks display capacity in **mAh (milliamp-hours)**, not Wh. Here's the formula:

**Wh = (mAh × V) ÷ 1000**

Most power banks use 3.7V cells. So:
- 10,000 mAh × 3.7V ÷ 1000 = **37 Wh** ✅ No problem
- 20,000 mAh × 3.7V ÷ 1000 = **74 Wh** ✅ No problem  
- 26,000 mAh × 3.7V ÷ 1000 = **96.2 Wh** ✅ Just under the limit
- 30,000 mAh × 3.7V ÷ 1000 = **111 Wh** ⚠️ Needs airline approval

**The practical rule of thumb:** Power banks under 27,000 mAh are generally fine without approval.

## Carry-On vs. Checked: Quick Reference

| Location | Allowed? |
|---|---|
| Carry-on bag | ✅ Yes (100 Wh limit without approval) |
| Checked luggage | ❌ Never permitted |
| On your person | ✅ Yes (same Wh limits apply) |

## Airline-Specific Notes

Most airlines follow the IATA guidelines exactly. Here are a few notable variations:

- **Emirates**: Allows up to 20 power banks in carry-on (though this is excessive for personal use)
- **Southwest Airlines**: Two free checked bags but power banks are still carry-on only
- **Qatar Airways**: Strictly enforces the 100 Wh limit at check-in and the gate

## Tips for Stress-Free Travel

1. **Write the Wh rating on the power bank** if it's not clearly printed
2. **Keep your power bank accessible** in your carry-on, not buried at the bottom — TSA may need to inspect it
3. **Charge it before your flight** — some airlines require batteries to be at a minimum charge level
4. **Don't pack two identical 100 Wh banks** hoping they'll be treated as one — each is assessed individually (which means two 100 Wh banks are fine, since each is under 100 Wh)

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I charge my power bank on a plane?**  
Only if the plane has USB ports or power outlets available, and using your own charging brick is typically fine. Some airlines restrict this.

**Can I bring multiple power banks?**  
Yes, as long as each one is under 100 Wh (or 101–160 Wh with approval). There's no set maximum number for sub-100 Wh banks, though you should only bring what you reasonably need.

**What if airport security confiscates my power bank?**  
If they attempt to put it in checked luggage, politely but firmly explain it cannot be checked — it must go in the cabin. If it exceeds 160 Wh, you may need to leave it behind or ship it.
